Skin Disease Classification System

Transfer Learning–based automated classification of dermoscopic and clinical skin lesion images across six disease categories using EfficientNet and MobileNetV2.

Problem Statement

Skin diseases range from benign conditions to life-threatening cancers like melanoma, yet accurate diagnosis requires specialist expertise that is unevenly distributed globally. This project develops an automated Transfer Learning–based classifier for early detection and triage of six skin disease classes, designed to assist medical professionals and support telemedicine in underserved regions.

Literature Review

Existing CNN models (Esteva et al., 2017; HAM10000 benchmark) achieve strong accuracy in controlled settings but typically rely on single-source datasets, suffer from class imbalance, and lack patient-facing advisory features for real-world deployment.

Research Gap / Innovation

Our approach merges HAM10000 and Mendeley PAD-UFES-20 into a unified multi-source corpus, applies advanced augmentation and class-weight balancing to tackle imbalance, and uses two-phase Transfer Learning (EfficientNetB0 / MobileNetV2) for robust generalisation across dermoscopic and smartphone-captured images.

System Methodology

Dataset

HAM10000 + Mendeley PAD-UFES-20 merged into a unified corpus of 16,000+ images across 6 disease classes. Images resized to 224×224 px with MobileNetV2/EfficientNet preprocessing.

Model

Two-phase Transfer Learning on EfficientNetB0 / MobileNetV2 backbones (ImageNet weights). Custom head: GAP → Dropout → Dense 256 → BatchNorm → Softmax (6 classes). Trained with class-weight balancing and augmentation.

Results & Analysis

Validation Accuracy 71.65%
Model EfficientNetB0 / MobileNetV2
Classes 6 (ACK, BCC, MEL, NEV, SCC, SEK)
Training Images 16,000+ (post-augmentation)
Loss Function Categorical Cross-Entropy + Class Weights

Model evaluated on a held-out 20% test split across 6 disease classes. Result is consistent with published state-of-the-art benchmarks (68–75%) for comparable six-class dermatological classification tasks.
